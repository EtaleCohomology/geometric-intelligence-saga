# The Geometry of Duration and the In-Between

*Bergson — Heidegger/Zollikon — Kimura/Kyōto — Corbin/Annales — and GI Theory.*

## Overview

The present subdirectory contains the six linguistic versions of a single essay: a working paper that traces the structural resonances between four intellectual traditions concerned with the temporal and spatial structure of human experience — Bergson's pure duration, Heidegger's being-in-the-world as articulated in the Zollikon seminars with Medard Boss, Kimura Bin's concept of the *aida* developed within the lineage of the Kyōto School, and Alain Corbin's history of sensibilities in the Annales tradition — and, on the other side, the mathematical apparatus of Geometric Intelligence theory: the differentiable manifold, the Lie derivative, scalar curvature, Morse functions, and geodesic distance.

The method adopted is neither translation nor formalisation nor reduction, but what the essay designates as *structural resonance* (Strukturresonanz): the reception of the points at which the mathematical and the philosophical, despite the radical incommensurability of their idioms, point toward the same structural phenomenon. The Lie derivative does not capture the essence of pure duration; the geodesic distance does not reconstitute Kimura's *aida*; nothing of the kind is claimed. What is claimed is only that, beneath these incommensurable surfaces, there runs a subterranean communication, and that the task of the essay is to attend to it wherever it can be heard.

The argument is framed by Nishida Kitarō's "absolutely contradictory self-identity" — the thesis that two things which contradict one another may, in the very midst of their contradiction, be one — as the final figure for the relation between mathematics and philosophy proposed by the essay. Two houses of being, in Heidegger's phrase, standing upon the same ground.

The closing pages extend the reflection to the Langlands programme, to Maruyama Yoshihiro's categorical unified science, and to Mochizuki's inter-universal Teichmüller theory, as open questions of twenty-first-century mathematics concerning the possibility of a new unity of knowledge: whether the modern division of intellectual labour, which tore apart the medieval map of the seven liberal arts, may yet be stitched together by the thread that the advances of these programmes have begun to suggest.

## On the Character of the Work

The present essay is, among the author's writings, the most personal. Although its argument is philosophical and mathematical throughout, the preface records the biographical experiences through which the author first encountered the themes that the essay takes up: the early recitation of Saint-Exupéry's *Le Petit Prince* in the French original before meaning could be grasped, the discovery of Paul Foulquié's *Précis de philosophie* in adolescence, the reading of Minkowski's *Le Temps vécu* and Merleau-Ponty's *Phénoménologie de la perception*, the encounter with the story of Nicolas Bourbaki, and the experience of growing up among Irish, English, American, French, Russian, Turkish and Orthodox Jewish companions — an experience within which the structural question that the essay poses was first encountered at the bodily level, before ever it took philosophical form.

In this respect, the work is not merely an essay upon its stated subject but also the author's intellectual autobiography: the record of the underground path by which a single human being came to believe that mathematics and the humanities might, in the life of one mind, cease to be strangers to one another.

## Genre

A working paper in the genre of the philosophical essay, drawing upon and in dialogue with twentieth-century phenomenology, psychopathology, historiography and mathematics. The work belongs to the lineage of Bergson's own essays, of the later Heidegger, and of the classical tradition of the philosophical meditation that refuses the separation of rigour and personal voice.
