*The Methodological Proposal.*

## Nature of the Study

The present study is a methodological proposal. It is composed upon a semi-synthetic dataset constructed by the author from musicological knowledge of the score: the eighteen variables per bar — treble and bass voicings, harmonic tension, chromatic density, texture, dynamic level, tonal distance, cadential strength, and so on — have been assigned upon the basis of a close reading of the published score, not extracted by automated analysis.

The study's principal claim is this: that upon a corpus of but forty-six bars, the four conditions of Proposition 2.1 of Geometric Intelligence theory are nevertheless satisfied, and that the resulting manifold admits of geometric analysis. In particular, the Lie derivative of the pullback metric attains its maximum at bar 34 — upon the text *cuius latus perforatum* — and the scalar curvature descends to a deep negative minimum across bars 31–36.

The empirical verification, in which the dataset is derived not from the author's musicological intuition but from the MIDI transcription of the score itself, is reserved to the companion study in `../part2_empirical/`.

## Multilingual Presentation

The five principal linguistic versions — Japanese, English, German, French, and Russian — are presented within a single document by way of a practical, rather than a dogmatic, decision. The study is of an introductory character, and it seemed to the author appropriate that a reader in any of the five languages might encounter the work as a whole, with the other languages visible alongside their own, rather than in a form editorially fragmented.

To these five principal languages are added, within the body of the text, citations and epigraphs in the liturgical and classical languages of the Western and Eastern Christian traditions: Latin — the language of the motet itself and of the author's own analytical vocabulary; Church Slavonic, whence is drawn the communion verse of the Divine Liturgy of St John Chrysostom, set in counterpoint to the Latin hymn; and Ancient Greek, for the epigraph from Plotinus' *Enneades* which opens the work. Italian translations of each epigraph accompany the principal five.

The empirical study of `../part2_empirical/` follows the more conventional form of separate files per language.
